About

My research explores how our cognitive system efficiently processes complex real-world environments with limited cognitive resources. This question always piques my curiosity and leads me to investigate it in various quantitative approaches.

I believe we can (and we should) study human cognition using quantitative methods. Regardless of how complex and subtle a cognitive function is, it corresponds to another format of measurable signals from humans, such as behaviour and brain activities. By measuring and analyzing these signals, we will be able to deeply comprehend the mechanisms of human cognition and eventually can simulate and predict the human.

To reach this goal, I have been studying various cognitive abilities including scene representational space, visual working memory, ensemble perception, aesthetic feelings, and so on (if you like, take a look at Projects!). Especially, my research focuses on biases or distortions produced during these cognitive processes. Although the biases or distortions sound like an inefficient process since they are against the veridical status of the external environments, they in fact represent the optimal outcome generated by our cognitive system while it tries to comprehend the world the best using its limited resources!
